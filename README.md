### Abstract
The ability to quantify bacterial abundance and activity within their populations and communities is foundational to microbiome research. However, the standard methods of direct cell counting with microscopy are often low throughput and time-consuming, with potentially low statistical power and user-to-user variability. Thus, many researchers rely instead on indirect tools, like optical density and quantitative PCR of the multicopy 16S rRNA gene, to estimate microbial community size. Furthermore, quantification of active cells is rarely performed despite an appreciable proportion of inactive cells present within microbial communities. Here, we build on traditional methods for bacterial cell staining and counting to optimize a high-throughput, largely automated, and precise protocol to collect informative parameters for single cells within microbial communities and populations. From these data, we quantify total cell counts and redox-active cell counts to determine community or population size, respiration activity, and morphological parameters. We demonstrate the high reproducibility and overall utility of this method applied on single strain-populations and high diversity soil communities. These information-rich phenotypic data provide fast, direct, and quantitative assessment of microbiomes, allowing researchers to obtain ecological and mechanistic insights from well-powered datasets.
